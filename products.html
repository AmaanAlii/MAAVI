PRECIOUS METAL​

Bullion is a term used to describe a bulk quantity of a precious metal. It is usually quantified by weight and has a fixed size. Bullion is often in the form of bars, ingots, or coins.

Bullion is usually at least 99.5% pure. It is often considered legal tender and is held in reserves by central banks. Institutional investors also use bullion to protect their portfolios from inflation.

Bullion can refer to raw metal or metal that has been processed into an object. For example, gold bullion is gold that has been refined to a high standard of purity. Silver bullion is silver that has been melted into bars or ingots.

Some say that gold is a safe investment because it tends to do well during economic downturns. However, gold is also volatile and can experience heavy losses.

TRADING

Spot prices are most frequently referenced in relation to the price of commodity futures contracts, such as contracts for oil, wheat, or gold. This is because stocks always trade at spot. You buy or sell a stock at the quoted price, and then exchange the stock for cash.

A futures contract price is commonly determined using the spot price of a commodity, expected changes in supply and demand, the risk-free rate of return for the holder of the commodity, and the costs of transportation or storage in relation to the maturity date of the contract. Futures contracts with longer times to maturity normally entail greater storage costs than contracts with nearby expiration dates.
